When my husband and I decided to hike the Appalachian Trail for our honeymoon, We needed to make sure we could take at least six months off of work, so we adjusted nearly every aspect of our spending. Instead of buying paper towels, we started using towels Little did we know, these money saving changes were actually the start of our low-waste lifestyle.

We don't buy things we don't need.

Gone are the days of frivilous spending and retail therapy, every purchase we make is a conscious one. We stick to the basics, with a few thoughtful treats here and there.

We use what we have.

We repair our belongings to extend their lifepan.

Before we toss anything in the garbage, we try to repair it first. We patch worn out clothing, mend broken XXX, and service appliances/machinary in order to keep them functioning as long as possible.

We opt for reusables.

Instead of wasting money on single-use items, we purchase things that can be use multiple times without needing a replacement. Making the switch to safety razors, cloth paper towels, reusable menstrual products, and refillable water bottles had a higher initial cost than their disposable counterparts, but have saved so much money in the long run.

We shop secondhand.

When there is something we need to buy, we can usually find it secondhand for a fraction of the cost. Last year, I saved thousands of dollars by thrifting my entire maternity closet and purchasing only previously used baby goods.

We are mindful of resource use.

Low-waste living isn't just about curbside garbage, it also requires being mindful of resources, like water and electricity. Making steps to reduce the amount of resources we use helps our monthly utility bills.
